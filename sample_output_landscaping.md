# Job Duties Suggestion: SOC 37-3011.00 (Landscaping and Groundskeeping Workers)

**Suggested job duty text:**

> Mow, trim, and plant; water, dig, and weed planting beds; lay sod; mulch beds,
> using mowers, string trimmers, hedge trimmers, blowers, shovels, rakes, and
> sprinkler/irrigation equipment.

**Avoid:**

- *Fertilizing/spraying*, also the defining task of Pesticide Handlers (37-3012.00),
  which pays ~24% more in our data ($23.79/hr vs $19.21/hr avg).
- *Supervisory language* ("oversee," "assign," "train"), risks reading as First-Line
  Supervisors of Landscaping (37-1012.00), a higher-tier SOC.
- *Tree-specialist language* ("climb," "chainsaw," "tree removal"), risks reading as
  Tree Trimmers and Pruners (37-3013.00), a distinct SOC.
- *Heavy equipment* (tractors, excavators, compactors), confirmed by a real RFI where
  a landscaping filer had to clarify heavy machinery is operated by a separate
  Equipment Operator position, not this one.

**Real RFI examples (this year, this SOC):**

- Vague "trimming" duties, DOL asked for specifics, filer named the tool: *"using
  the weedeater... tools to be used would be shovel."*
- "Pulling a gooseneck trailer," DOL flagged it, filer clarified: *"operate a
  pickup truck."*
- Tree-trimming duties, DOL asked if it reached above ground, filer clarified a
  separate "Tree Climber" role handles that, this one stays ground-level.

**Basis:** 1,179 real H-2B filings for this SOC, cross-checked against O*NET's
official task list. Every suggested duty appears in 50%+ of real filings and is an
officially recognized core task; tool examples are the most frequently named real
tools for this SOC, cross-checked against O*NET. Tool specificity was added based on
the team's own observation that generic "tools" language is a leading RFI trigger,
confirmed in our data (84% of RFIs mention tool/equipment specifically).

### 2. Suggested Job Duty Text

**Basis:** every duty included appears in more than 50% of real filings for this
SOC. Nine phrases cleared that bar, so the "at least 5, or whichever is greater"
floor rule wasn't needed here. Cross-checked against O*NET's official Task list for
37-3011.00 (27 listed tasks): all 9 suggested duties map directly to explicitly
recognized O*NET tasks for this occupation, so this isn't just "common," it's
officially core.

No hedge words ("may," "typically," "such as," "including but not limited to").
Every clause is a direct duty statement.

**Tool specificity (added per team feedback):** the team's own RFI experience this
year points to generic tool/equipment language ("hand and power tools") as one of
the most common triggers for a DOL request for clarification. In our data, 84% of
RFI-flagged filings mention "tool" or "equipment" specifically. Rather than a
generic phrase, the tool examples in the suggested text above are named
individually, selected from two combined sources:

- **Real filing frequency** (how often each specific tool is named in real
  landscaping job duties text): sprinkler/irrigation equipment 49.6%, rakes 39.2%,
  shovels 26.5%, mowers 23.7%, blowers 21.8%, string trimmers 20.0%, hedge
  trimmers/shears 16.3%.
- **O*NET's official task descriptions**, which independently name the same
  categories of tools/equipment for this SOC.

These are meant as a short, generic, illustrative list, not exhaustive and not a
literal inventory of what a specific employer owns. Notably, riskier or more
specialized tools (chainsaws, tractors, mini-excavators, compaction equipment) were
deliberately left out, both because they're less common in the aggregate data and
because of the SOC-boundary risk covered below.

### 3. Risk Summary

**Confirmed risk, backed by real filing data on both sides**

**37-3012.00 Pesticide Handlers, Sprayers, and Applicators, Vegetation** is one of
O*NET's own listed "Related Occupations" for landscaping, and it's also one of our
26 target SOC codes, so we have real filings on both sides to compare:

- Pesticide Handlers average **$23.79/hr** starting wage vs. Landscaping's
  **$19.21/hr**, a ~24% gap in our data.
- 100% of Pesticide Handler filings with duty text mention chemical/spray/pesticide
  language; 60% mention licensing or certification requirements.
- The overlapping task is real: "mix and spray or spread fertilizers, herbicides, or
  insecticides" is literally listed as an official O*NET task for *landscaping* too,
  but it's also the defining task of the higher-paying Pesticide Handler
  classification.

**This is why "fertilizing/spraying" was deliberately left out of the suggested
text**, even though it's a genuine O*NET-recognized landscaping task and appears in
real filings (47.9%, just under our threshold anyway). Including it would use
language that overlaps with a specific, higher-wage, adjacent SOC that we can
concretely prove is real risk, not a hypothetical one.

**Other risk patterns found in real landscaping filings, excluded from the suggestion**

- **Supervisory language** ("supervise," "oversee," "train," "assign") appears in
  19.3% of real landscaping filings, but risks reading as **37-1012.00 First-Line
  Supervisors of Landscaping, Lawn Service, and Groundskeeping Workers**, a distinct
  related O*NET occupation typically compensated at a supervisor tier. Excluded.
- **Tree-specialist language** ("climb," "chainsaw," "tree removal") appears in 4.8%
  of real landscaping filings, but risks reading as **37-3013.00 Tree Trimmers and
  Pruners**, a distinct related SOC. Excluded.
- **Heavy equipment** (tractors, mini-excavators, compaction equipment): this isn't
  a hypothetical risk. A real RFI in our data (case H-400-26001-522409) shows a
  landscaping filer explicitly clarifying: *"Our Right of Way Laborer/Landscapers do
  not operate heavy machinery... any heavy tractors/equipment are operated by our
  certified Equipment Operators."* That's a direct, real confirmation that naming
  heavy equipment risks reading as **47-2073.00 Operating Engineers and Other
  Construction Equipment Operators**, not a wage-data inference but an actual DOL
  exchange. Excluded from the suggested tool list for exactly this reason.

Notably, both of these already fall well below the 50% threshold on their own. The
frequency-based approach is already doing a lot of the defensive work by itself,
before any deliberate risk-based judgment is applied. The O*NET-related-occupation
check adds the *evidence for why* those exclusions matter, rather than the cutoff
being an arbitrary number.

### 4. Real RFI Examples (for reference: limited language, not exhaustive)

Actual DOL requests for clarification on real landscaping filings, trimmed to the
essential exchange (case numbers included for traceability, no employer-identifying
details). These aren't meant to be exhaustive, just illustrative of the kind of
follow-up that's actually happening, so a filer knows what to expect if similar
language is used.

**Tool specificity:**
> RFI asked what "trimming" and related duties actually involved. Response:
> *"Trimming refers to using the weedeater to trim the grass... Tools to be used
> would be shovel. If ground is too hard for shovel, I would use machine to dig
> hole."* (Case H-400-26004-532827)

**Vehicle/equipment clarification:**
> RFI flagged a mention of "operating equipment that involves pulling the gooseneck
> trailer." Response: *"Operate a pickup truck."* (Case H-400-26002-529531)

**SOC-boundary clarification:**
> RFI asked about tree-trimming duties reaching above ground level. Response: *"All
> above ground tree trimming duties are performed by separate workers in the
> position of Tree Climber. [This role] assist[s] only in trimming vegetation from
> the ground."* (Case H-400-26001-522409)

These three patterns (vague tool/equipment language, vehicle/equipment mentions
needing clarification, and duties that brush up against a different, more
specialized classification) line up directly with what the Risk Summary above is
designed to catch before a filing is ever submitted.

### Key takeaway for team review

The frequency threshold and the O*NET cross-checks aren't redundant; they catch
different things. Frequency alone would have quietly dropped fertilizing/spraying,
supervisory language, and tree-specialist language just for being uncommon, without
ever explaining *why* that's actually the right call. The Risk Summary turns that
into an explicit, evidence-backed reason tied to a specific adjacent SOC and (where
we have the data) a real wage gap, which is exactly the kind of justification that
should hold up if a filing ever gets questioned.

The RFI examples add a fourth layer of evidence that isn't inference at all: it's
what DOL actually asked, in this SOC, this year. The tool-specificity finding in
particular (84% of RFIs mention tool/equipment) came directly from the team's own
field experience and was then confirmed in our own data, which is exactly the kind
of feedback loop this tool should keep running on as more real outcomes come in.
